Your First Wedding — Third Shooting for Free

The Three Wedding Rule

Third shoot at least three weddings with three different experienced photographers before booking your own clients.

Why three:

Each photographer has a different style, system, and approach.

One wedding teaches you basics.

Three weddings gives you a real education.

Wedding 1:

Focus on exposure.

Learn your camera in a real environment.

Stay out of the way.

Watch everything.

Wedding 2:

Focus on posing and directing.

Watch how the primary guides couples.

Try directing during low stakes moments.

Cocktail hour is perfect for this.

Wedding 3:

Focus on the full day timeline.

How does an experienced photographer manage getting ready through reception?

How do they handle problems when they come up?

What does a real wedding day actually feel like start to finish?

After three weddings:

You have a real portfolio.

You have real experience.

You have enough confidence to book your first paying client.

Why Third Shooting

Real world experience under an experienced photographer is worth more than any guide.

Zero pressure. Real light. Real moments.

The primary photographer has everything covered. You're free to learn.

How to Find a Photographer to Shadow

Search Instagram and Google for wedding photographers in your area.

Look for photographers shooting 30 to 50 weddings a year. Busy enough to want help. Not so large they have a full team already.

Send a direct message or email:

"Hi, I'm learning wedding photography and I'd love to third shoot one of your weddings for free. I'll stay out of your way, follow your lead, and only need permission to use any photos I take for my own portfolio. No cost to you. No obligation beyond that one wedding."

Most photographers will say yes. It costs them nothing and having an extra set of eyes is always useful.

Before the Wedding

Be fully honest with the photographer about your experience level. They need to know so they can guide you.

The couple does not need to know.

Ask the photographer ahead of time:

What is your preferred communication style during the wedding — hand signals, whispers, text?

Are there moments where you want me to hang back completely?

Can I check in with you quietly during transitions?

Getting Guidance Without Giving Yourself Away

During transitions — cocktail hour setup, moving from ceremony to portraits — pull the photographer aside quietly and ask questions then.

Text message — if you need guidance and can't pull them aside a quick text is invisible to guests.

Watch and mirror — if unsure what to do watch what the primary photographer is doing and shoot the same moment from a different angle.

Phrases that work:

Instead of "I don't know what to do next" say "What's the priority for the next 20 minutes?"

Instead of "I've never done this before" say "Walk me through your timeline for the rest of the day."

Guests never pay attention to photographers. You are invisible to them. Relax and shoot.

Experimenting with Manual Exposure

Third shooting is the perfect time to experiment with manual exposure in a real environment with zero pressure.

Start during cocktail hour — low stakes, guests mingling, no critical moments.

Lock these first:

Shutter: 1/180

ISO: 400

Aperture: f/4

Then adjust ONE element at a time and watch what happens:

Bump ISO to 800 — image gets brighter. Everything else stays the same.

Open aperture to f/2.8 — image gets brighter. Background gets softer.

Close aperture to f/5.6 — image gets darker. More in focus front to back.

Speed shutter to 1/250 — image gets darker.

Slow shutter to 1/100 — image gets brighter. Risk of blur increases.

Adjust one element at a time. By end of cocktail hour you will have a physical feel for how each element interacts that no guide can teach you.

What to Shoot

Shoot everything the primary photographer shoots from a different angle. You are building a portfolio of real wedding moments.

Stay invisible to guests. You are a shadow not a presence.

Key moments to focus on:

Getting ready details — dress, shoes, jewelry, rings

Ceremony — processional, vows, first kiss, recessional

Portraits — watch how the primary directs couples

Reception — first dance, toasts, candid moments, dancing

Operational Awareness

Always be aware of your equipment status throughout the wedding day. This is as important as composition and exposure.

Check batteries:

Glance at battery icon in viewfinder before every major transition — getting ready to ceremony, ceremony to portraits, portraits to reception.

Never let a battery die mid-wedding. Change at 20% or earlier.

Check card space:

Monitor shot count in viewfinder. 128GB cards hold approximately 1,200-1,500 RAW images.

Change cards at 900-1,000 images to stay safe. Change during transitions only — never during key moments.

Know where you are in the timeline:

Ask the primary photographer for a rough timeline at the start of the day.

Know when cocktail hour starts, when portraits end, when reception begins.

This helps you anticipate when to change cards, check batteries, or setup lighting.

Watch the primary photographer:

Notice when they change lenses, swap cards, or adjust settings.

This teaches you the rhythm of a real wedding day that no guide can teach.

After the Wedding

Edit your best 20 images. That is your portfolio starting point.

Back up everything the same night. Never wait until morning.
